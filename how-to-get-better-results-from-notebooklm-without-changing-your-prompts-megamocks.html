How to Get Better Results From NotebookLM Without Changing Your Prompts

How to Get Better Results From NotebookLM Without Changing Your Prompts is something most people never figure out — and at MegaMocks, we cover the practical stuff that actually makes a difference.

Most people open NotebookLM, throw in some files, type a prompt, and get a bland answer. Then they try a better prompt. Still bland. The problem isn't the prompt. It never was. The problem is everything that happens before you type anything. There are four steps that shape every answer NotebookLM gives you. Miss any one of them, and the results stay generic no matter what you type.

Here's what those four steps actually look like.

Step 1: Be Picky About What You Upload

NotebookLM works nothing like ChatGPT. When you ask ChatGPT something, it pulls from its training data. You have no say in that. NotebookLM only pulls from the files you give it. Every answer comes directly from your documents — nothing outside of that.

So whatever goes in controls what comes out.

The most common mistake is treating the notebook like a dump folder. People grab a bunch of PDFs, throw them all in, and start chatting. The answers they get are vague and kind of useless — because the AI is working with a small, unchecked pile of documents.

The fix is simple: be selective. Only upload sources you've actually read or confirmed are relevant to the specific project this notebook is for.

NotebookLM also has a built-in web research tool. Under "Add Sources," you'll find a Web option with two modes — fast research and deep research. Fast research does a quick scan and grabs a handful of links. Deep research runs in the background, browses hundreds of pages, and returns a full report with recommended sources.

Pro tip: Tell deep research what to include and what to skip. Something like — "Research product launch strategies for B2B SaaS companies. Focus on industry reports, case studies, and data from consulting firms. Exclude blog posts, opinion pieces, and listicles." That one extra line filters out the junk before it even shows up.

Even after deep research returns results, don't import blindly. Open each source, skim it, decide if it belongs. Takes five extra minutes. But a clean notebook is what gives you answers you can trust later.

Once your sources are in, run this check: "Are there any contradictions or conflicting data points across my sources?" NotebookLM will scan everything and flag where documents disagree. Catch those early and every answer from that point forward is built on solid ground.

Step 2: Configure the Notebook Before You Ask Anything

This is the step almost nobody does. And it's exactly why most notebooks produce generic answers.

Click "Configure Notebook" after your sources are loaded. You'll see two settings.

The first is the conversational goal. Three options: Default (general purpose), Learning Guide (educational framing), and Custom. Always pick Custom. This is where you tell NotebookLM how to think — what role to play, what to focus on, what actually matters for your project.

For a product launch notebook, you might write: "Act as a product marketing strategist preparing a B2B SaaS launch. Prioritize competitive positioning, messaging clarity, pricing sensitivity, and go-to-market sequencing."

The second setting is response length. Research and analysis work? Go longer. Daily briefing notebook where you just want highlights? Go shorter.

Save those settings. Then ask the exact same question you would've asked before. The difference is obvious. Instead of five bullet points that could describe any product anywhere, you get answers that pull from actual competitor data in your sources and connect to your specific goal.

Not sure how to write the custom role? Ask NotebookLM before you configure anything: "Based on the sources in this notebook, suggest three custom roles I should configure for the most useful responses." It reads your documents and recommends roles that actually fit. Way better than guessing.

Step 3: Use the ACG Workflow to Stress-Test Your Answers

A good setup gets you a solid first answer. But a first answer is still a first draft. And first drafts have blind spots that show up at the worst time — like in the middle of a meeting.

The ACG workflow catches them before that happens. It stands for Analyze, Challenge, Gap.

Analyze is what most people already do. Ask NotebookLM to pull insights from your sources. That's your starting point.

Challenge is where it gets useful. After you get your first answer, type: "What are the weakest assumptions in this analysis and which claims have the least supporting evidence?" This makes NotebookLM push back on its own output. It might flag that a pricing argument doesn't account for enterprise buyers, or that a positioning angle relies on claims that aren't backed up well. These are the exact questions someone in a meeting would ask. Now you have the answers ready.

Gap is the last one. Type: "What's missing from my sources? What topics or data points would I need to make this analysis complete?" NotebookLM will tell you what's not there — missing market data, no buyer persona research, whatever it is. That one prompt tells you exactly what to upload next. Your notebook gets stronger every session instead of staying the same.

Two other things in the chat panel worth knowing.

Citations. Every response has small reference markers. Hover over one and you see the exact quote from the exact source behind that claim. You can verify anything in a couple of seconds. General chatbots can't do that — they pull from training data you can't see.

Source filtering. On the left side, every source has a checkmark. Those checkmarks control which documents the chat pulls from. If you only want answers from your beta user survey, uncheck everything else and ask. Targeted answer, not a blended summary.

And here's a move worth building into your routine: when you get an answer you want to keep, save it as a note. Then click the three dots and hit "Convert to Source." That answer now sits alongside your original documents. Next time you ask something, NotebookLM draws from your own analysis on top of the raw data. The notebook compounds on itself. Every good answer you save makes the next one better.

Step 4: Use Studio to Turn Conversations Into Real Deliverables

Everything up to this point is conversation. At some point you need actual documents — something to send, present, or share. That's what the Studio panel is for.

Look for a small toggle on the right side of the screen. Studio takes everything in your notebook — sources, saved notes, configured settings — and builds finished outputs from it.

There are a lot of output types. But three are worth focusing on.

Audio Overviews generate an AI podcast — two hosts talking through your source material. The default version gives a general overview. Before you generate, click the pencil icon. It opens format options: Deep Dive, Debate, Critique, Brief. Pick the one that fits. You can also add a focus instruction — something like "Focus on whether our pricing model is competitive based on the market data, and challenge any assumptions in the beta user feedback." The hosts will argue different positions using data from your notebook. There's even an interactive mode where you can jump in mid-conversation, ask a question, and they'll answer from the notebook before picking back up.

Reports give you formatted documents. Preset options like briefing doc sit at the top. Below those, NotebookLM suggests formats based on what's actually in your notebook — not generic templates, but formats matched to your content. Click one and it generates a multi-page document pulling from every source. The kind of thing that used to take an hour and a half manually now takes about 90 seconds. If none of the presets fit, click "Create Your Own" and describe what you need.

Data Tables are probably the most underrated feature in the whole tool. Ask NotebookLM to build a comparison table — say, all three competitors with columns for pricing tiers, free trial length, onboarding support, and biggest weaknesses. It pulls from across all your sources, organizes everything into a clean table, and you can export it directly to Google Sheets in about a minute.

You can also share your notebook. Full access lets someone add sources and chat with the notebook themselves. Chat-only access means they can ask questions but can't change anything. You build it once, configure it right, and anyone you share it with pulls from the same clean setup.

The Setup Is the Prompt

Most people spend all their time trying to write better prompts. Prompts matter — but they're not the main variable.

The quality of your results is mostly decided before you type a single word. Clean sources, a configured role, the ACG workflow, and the right output format — those four things shape everything NotebookLM produces. Get those right and the prompts almost write themselves.
