📋 PYQ Strategy

Why Previous Year Questions Are the Most Underrated Exam Prep Tool

📖 8 min readExam PrepJanuary 2025

Ask a student who scored in the top 5% of their exam what they did differently. Most of them will mention previous year questions. Ask a student who underperformed what they wish they'd done more of. Most of them will say the same thing.

Previous year questions — PYQs — are consistently one of the highest-return activities a student can do in the weeks before an exam. Yet a significant number of students either skip them entirely or treat them as an afterthought. This post explains why that's a mistake and how to use PYQs properly.

What Are Previous Year Questions?

Previous year questions are actual exam papers from past sittings of the same exam — or practice papers set in the same style and format as the actual exam. They're available for most board exams, university courses, and competitive entrance tests.

Why PYQs Work So Well

They show you exactly what the exam tests

Textbooks and notes cover far more content than any single exam paper can test. PYQs reveal which topics and question types actually appear with regularity — and which ones are rarely or never tested. This is incredibly useful for prioritisation. A topic that has appeared in seven of the last ten years' papers is almost certainly worth more of your time than one that appeared once five years ago.

They train you on question formats

Exams have specific ways of asking questions. Some subjects always ask for step-by-step working. Others want structured arguments. Others use very specific command words — "evaluate," "compare," "explain the significance of" — that require distinct types of answers. PYQs familiarise you with the format so you're not encountering it for the first time on exam day.

They build exam-condition performance

There's a difference between knowing material and being able to access it quickly under time pressure, with limited information, and real stakes. That skill is built through practice under exam conditions. Students who have done 10 full papers under timed conditions are dramatically better at managing time and composure on the real day than those who haven't.

They expose gaps you didn't know you had

You can feel confident about a topic until a past paper question asks about it in a way that reveals you only understood it superficially. PYQs are the most realistic test available of whether your preparation is actually solid.

How to Use PYQs Properly

Attempt before checking answers

Always try to answer the question before looking at the solution. The attempt — even an incorrect one — engages your brain actively with the problem. Just reading the solution without attempting the question first is much less effective.

Review mistakes carefully

When you get a question wrong, understand why. Was it a content gap? A misreading of the question? A calculation error? A time management issue? Different causes have different fixes, and identifying the cause is essential to actually improving.

Track your coverage

The PYQ Coverage Tracker lets you record how many questions you've solved per subject and calculates your coverage percentage. Aim for 70%+ coverage across all subjects. Below 50% in any subject is a clear signal to prioritise more past paper practice there.

Do full papers under timed conditions

Solving individual questions has value, but it doesn't replicate the experience of managing time across a full paper. Once you're 4–6 weeks from your exam, aim to do at least 2–3 full past papers under proper timed conditions before the real exam.

How Many Years of PYQs Should You Do?

For most exams, the last 5–7 years gives you a solid picture of the question patterns. For very competitive exams with larger question banks, 10 years is better. Focus on the most recent years first — exam style and content emphasis can shift over time.

A Note on Using PYQs Too Early

PYQs are most effective once you have a reasonable base of content knowledge. Attempting past papers before you've covered the relevant syllabus is demotivating and less productive — you can't answer questions on material you haven't studied. Use PYQs as a consolidation and testing tool, not a first-pass learning tool.

Past papers don't predict the future perfectly — but they predict it better than anything else you have access to.
