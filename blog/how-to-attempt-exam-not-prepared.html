How to Attempt an Exam You're Not Fully Prepared For and Still Pass

📖 9 min read Exam Strategy June 2025

In this article, I will be covering everything related to how to attempt an exam when you are not fully prepared and still pass it. This particular strategy will change the way you look at exam day and honestly it might just save you from failing. So if you want to know how to handle pressure, manage your attempt smartly and still come out with a passing score then read this article till end.

Hello guys! What's up? My name is Prince Upadhyay and welcome to MegaMocks, your go-to place for exam prep tools and study strategies. So let's start with the topic...

So since exams are not just about how much you studied so they are also about how smartly you used what you knew on that particular day. A lot of people go into exams having covered maybe 60 or 70 percent of the syllabus and then they completely fall apart inside the exam hall because they did not know how to handle the rest. And then they come out and say things like oh I was not prepared and all that. The reason is simple, they treated the exam like a memory dump instead of treating it like a strategy game.

In addition, a lot of people have this one specific fear which is that if I don't know something I should just leave it or I should just sit there staring at it. And that particular mindset is what kills most scores honestly. The exam does not care how you felt going in. It only cares about what you wrote and what you attempted and what you left blank.

In this article I won't be teaching you time management for the entire month of preparation which I have already covered in other articles over here on MegaMocks. So do check those out but later on. The main focus over here is that one day. That particular day. The day you walk in not fully ready and you still have to perform.

The Circle Pass: Your First Five Minutes

So the first thing you need to understand is that you do not need to know everything to pass. You know? A lot of exams are designed so that even if you get 55 to 60 percent right you clear them and that is the whole game actually. So when you sit down and the question paper is in front of you the first instinct most people have is to start from question one and go straight down like a robot. Do not do that. What you want to do is spend the first — and actually I remember the first time I did this I felt weird doing it but it genuinely worked — spend the first five to seven minutes just flipping through and circling the questions you actually know. That particular round is your confidence map and it shows you right in front of you where your marks are sitting.

Now once you have done that pass of the paper you go back and you do those questions first. Why? Because the brain works in a really specific way and when you solve things you know first you get into a flow and that flow actually helps you think better for the harder stuff later. It is kind of like how when you are warming up at the gym and the first two sets feel impossible but then by the fourth set your body is just doing it. Same thing happens with your brain inside an exam.

Elimination Over Guessing: The MCQ Advantage

The second thing is about partial marks and eliminations and this particular thing is so underused it is crazy. So since most objective exams have negative marking so a lot of students just leave questions they are not sure about. But the thing is there is a difference between completely not knowing and being able to eliminate one or two wrong options. If a question has four options and you can confidently remove two of them then your chances are 50 50 and that is not guessing that is calculating. I generally use this method in every MCQ exam I have ever given and it has never hurt me more than it has helped me honestly.

Now there is a really important distinction over here between educated elimination and random picking. Educated elimination is when you can actually reason out why a particular option is wrong and all that. Random picking is just closing your eyes and going for it. The first one is a legitimate exam strategy and the second one is just luck. So since you have studied 60 to 70 percent of the syllabus so even on topics you did not fully cover there will be options that look obviously wrong to you. Use that instinct.

Protecting Your Prepared Chapters

Third thing is managing the chapters you studied well versus the ones you barely touched. So what you want to do is make sure that in the chapters you did prepare well you are not making careless mistakes. Those are your guaranteed marks and you cannot afford to mess those up just because you are rushing or nervous or thinking about the chapter you did not study and all that. Stay inside those questions. Read them twice. Be accurate over there. The unprepared chapters will come and go but your prepared chapters are where you build the floor of your score.

And honestly this particular thing sounds obvious but a lot of students do the opposite. They spend too much time on hard questions they are unlikely to get and then they rush through the easy ones they should have locked in. That particular mistake costs more marks than not knowing the hard questions in the first place.

Writing Partial Answers in Subjective Exams

The fourth thing is about subjective or long answer exams specifically. So if it is a written exam and you genuinely do not know the full answer to a question what you do is you write what you know confidently and you structure it well. Markers are humans — and by the way this is something most coaching institutes will never tell you because they want you to study everything — markers are humans and they give partial credit when they see that a student understood the concept even if they did not nail every detail. So write what you know, use proper headings if the format allows it, and make it look organised. That particular presentation matters a lot more than most students think.

Also never leave a subjective question completely blank if you have any related knowledge at all. Even a definition of the topic, even a basic formula that connects to it. Something on the page is always better than nothing because a blank page is a guaranteed zero and even a partial attempt has a chance.

The 80-20 Time Rule Inside the Hall

I generally use two mental checklists, first one is the circle pass which I described over here where you go through and mark what you know before writing anything, and second one is the 80 20 rule which means 80 percent of your time should go into the 80 percent of the paper you can actually attempt and only 20 percent of your time goes into the hard stuff.

The circle pass helps you see your opportunities clearly right from the start so you do not waste the first twenty minutes on a question you have no idea about.

The 80 20 rule keeps you from getting stuck. Because getting stuck is the silent mark killer in every exam and a lot of students lose 15 to 20 marks just by spending too long on questions they were never going to get right anyway. Set a time limit per question in your head and when that limit is up you move on. You can always come back if time allows.

The Logic Behind All of This

Now let me explain the logic behind all of this. Why? Because at the end of the day an exam under-preparation is a resource management problem not a knowledge problem anymore. The knowledge window is closed. You studied what you studied and that is done. What is still open is how you distribute your time, your confidence and your attempt order inside those two or three hours. And how is it so? Because marks do not care which question you answered first. They only count the correct ones. So since you cannot add knowledge in the exam hall so the only thing you can add is strategy and that is what separates people who just barely pass from people who fail even after studying a lot.

Inside the exam hall, strategy is the only resource you can still add. Use it like it is the most valuable thing you brought in.
