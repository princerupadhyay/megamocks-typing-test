Google's Android AI Is So Good It Made an iPhone Guy Want to Switch

Google dropped some Android AI updates this week that almost convinced a 15-year iPhone user to switch phones. Not almost — he said he's actually considering it. That's how wild things have gotten. Here at MegaMocks, we keep tabs on AI news that actually matters to real people, and this week had a lot to talk about.

People Are Criticizing AI Art That Isn't Actually AI Art

Before the tech news, there's something that blew up online this week and it's worth talking about.

A guy on X posted an image and told everyone he made it with AI — in the style of a Monet painting. He asked people to explain why it was worse than the real thing.

The comments went in hard. People said the colors were off. No emotion. Harsh. Flat. Just not good enough.

Except it was a real Monet. He cropped into an actual painting and lied about where it came from. After he came clean, a ton of those comments quietly disappeared.

This is AI hysteria in action. Some people are so ready to hate anything AI-related that they'll trash a genuine Monet — just because they were told a machine made it. That's not a take on technology. That's just bias doing the thinking for you.

Worth keeping in mind next time you feel the urge to dismiss something the second you hear "AI" attached to it.

Android's New AI Features Are Actually Kind of Insane

Google held their Android keynote this week. It was heavy on AI — probably the most AI-focused one they've done.

The big announcement was Gemini Intelligence. It's not a chat app. It's an AI layer that runs across your whole Android setup — phone, watch, and eventually glasses. Everything talks to each other and can handle multi-step tasks on your behalf.

They brought Paris Hilton on stage to demo it. She got a text asking about dinner Thursday at 7 PM. A prompt popped up asking if she wanted to add it to her calendar. She tapped yes. Then she asked Google out loud what the Italian place was that her mom mentioned last week. Google pulled it from her old messages and gave her the name. She said to send a message saying let's go there. Google drafted it. She said send. It went.

Hands-free. No opening five apps. No copy-pasting. It pulled from Gmail, her messages, and her calendar all at once.

That's the kind of AI experience people have been asking for. It just works, and it doesn't require a manual.

A couple more Android features worth knowing:

Rambler is a voice dictation tool that doesn't just write what you say — it figures out what you meant to say. You can go back and give it instructions like "make this more professional" and it adjusts. It even handles multiple languages in the same message, so if you switch between English and Spanish mid-sentence, it keeps up.

Custom Widgets let you describe what you want on your home screen and the AI builds it. No coding. It just shows up.

There's also an auto-fill feature that reads your photos, Gmail, and messages to fill in forms for you automatically. Their example was a passport photo — if it's saved in your camera roll, Android can pull the info and fill in travel forms automatically.

ChatGPT Has New Tricks Too

OpenAI had two big updates this week.

The first one is Personal Finance. You can now connect ChatGPT to personal finance apps. It'll show you a breakdown of your investments, flag where you're overspending, list upcoming subscriptions, and even suggest which ones you might want to cancel. You can just ask it questions about your money and get real answers back.

Heads up: Connecting your bank accounts to an AI is a big step. Even people who are fully bought in on AI have paused on this one. Think it through carefully before you connect anything sensitive.

The second update is Codex on mobile. Codex is OpenAI's AI coding agent, similar to tools like Claude Code. Now you can access your Codex projects from the ChatGPT app on your phone. If your computer is on and Codex is running, you can check in on your projects from anywhere.

Does this make Codex better than Claude's tools for everyday people? Not really. Codex still skews technical. If you're not a developer, it can feel like you're missing context. Claude's setup is a bit more approachable for people who just want to get things done without writing code. This Week's Prompt Trick: QR Code Wallpapers

This one's a creative use of ChatGPT that's genuinely clever.

The creator was heading to Google IO and wanted to share his YouTube channel without handing out a boring black-and-white QR code. So he uploaded a photo of his dogs, asked ChatGPT to turn them into a cartoon, and had the QR code woven into the carpet of the image. The result was a phone wallpaper that doubles as a working QR code.

You can do the same thing. Upload any photo, give ChatGPT the URL you want, and ask it to blend a QR code into the scene naturally. Tell it the style you want — cartoon, watercolor, sketch — and it'll go from there.

It sounds like a gimmick but it's actually pretty useful. A QR code that looks like real art gets scanned. A plain QR code gets ignored.

A Few More Updates From Google

Google wasn't done after the Android keynote.

Gmail's Help Me Write feature is getting smarter for Workspace users. It'll soon pull from your past emails and Google Drive files to match your tone when drafting new messages. So instead of generic AI-sounding email drafts, it sounds more like you. Not available for regular Gmail yet, but hopefully it gets there.

Stitch — Google's AI tool for mocking up app and website designs — got a visual upgrade too. Images it generates now look noticeably better and fit brand styles more cleanly. If you've tried it before and weren't impressed, it's worth another look.

And Grok added connectors for both web and mobile. You can now hook it up to your apps and use them inside chats, similar to what ChatGPT has been doing for a while.

So Where Does This Leave Us?

It's not a small thing when a 15-year iPhone user says he's thinking about switching. That's not hype. That's someone looking at what Android is actually doing with AI right now and thinking — okay, maybe they're ahead.

Apple has promised a lot of AI features. A lot of them still aren't here. Meanwhile Android is showing up with tools that actually connect your life together without a lot of setup or fuss.

Things are moving fast. The gap between platforms can close or open in a single announcement. If you want to keep up with all of it, come back to MegaMocks.
